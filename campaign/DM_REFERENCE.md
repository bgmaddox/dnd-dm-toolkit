# DM Reference — D&D 5e 2024 Rules

This document is a Claude-facing reference. It captures key rules, DM rulings philosophy,
and practical guidance relevant to running sessions. Update as the campaign evolves.

---

## System: D&D 5e 2024 (5.5e)

The 2024 Player's Handbook is a revision of the 2014 edition. Key changes relevant to this campaign:

### Core Rule Changes
- **Weapon Mastery:** Each weapon has a mastery property (Cleave, Graze, Nick, Push, Sap, Slow, Topple, Vex).
  Characters can use a limited number of these per turn based on class.
- **Species (formerly Race):** Racial ability score increases removed; backgrounds now provide ASIs (+2/+1 or +1/+1/+1).
- **Revised Backgrounds:** Each background grants an ability score increase, a feat, skill/tool proficiencies.
- **Revised Spell Lists:** Spells are now on class lists (not school lists). Some spells moved or revised.
- **Lucky Feat:** Revised — can now be used after seeing the outcome, not before.
- **Unarmed Strikes:** Now a Light weapon, can dual-wield fist + weapon.
- **Jump:** Now a bonus action (uses movement, no action required).
- **Shove/Grapple:** Now Attack action options (replace one attack), not separate actions.
- **Exhaustion:** Simplified — each level = -1 to d20 tests and spell save DCs.

### Conditions (Quick Reference)
| Condition | Key Effect |
|-----------|-----------|
| Blinded | Auto-fail sight checks; attacks against = advantage; your attacks = disadvantage |
| Charmed | Can't attack charmer; charmer has social advantage |
| Deafened | Auto-fail hearing checks |
| Exhaustion | -1 per level to all d20 rolls; death at level 6 |
| Frightened | Disadvantage on checks/attacks while source visible; can't move toward source |
| Grappled | Speed 0; ends if grappler incapacitated or target moved out of range |
| Incapacitated | No actions or reactions |
| Invisible | Attacks against = disadvantage; your attacks = advantage; can't be seen |
| Paralyzed | Incapacitated + can't move/speak; attacks = auto-crit if within 5 ft |
| Petrified | Incapacitated, no movement, auto-fail Str/Dex saves, resistance to all damage |
| Poisoned | Disadvantage on attack rolls and ability checks |
| Prone | Disadvantage on attacks; attacks against = advantage if within 5 ft, else disadvantage |
| Restrained | Speed 0; attacks = disadvantage; attacks against = advantage; Dex saves = disadvantage |
| Stunned | Incapacitated, can't move; auto-fail Str/Dex saves; attacks against = advantage |
| Unconscious | Incapacitated + prone + auto-fail Str/Dex; attacks = auto-crit within 5 ft |

### Action Economy
- **Action:** Attack, Cast a Spell, Dash, Disengage, Dodge, Help, Hide, Search, Use an Object
- **Bonus Action:** Only if a feature, spell, or item specifically grants one
- **Reaction:** Only if a feature grants one (opportunity attack, Shield spell, Counterspell, etc.)
- **Free Object Interaction:** One per turn (draw/sheathe weapon, open door)
- **Opportunity Attack:** Triggered when a creature leaves your reach without Disengaging. Uses your Reaction.

### Advantage & Disadvantage
- **Advantage:** Roll 2d20, take the higher result. Granted by favorable conditions, certain spells, or flanking (optional rule).
- **Disadvantage:** Roll 2d20, take the lower result. Imposed by unfavorable conditions, debuffs, or conditions like Poisoned, Frightened, Restrained.
- **Cancellation:** One source of advantage + one source of disadvantage = straight roll, regardless of how many sources of each exist.
- **Cannot stack:** Multiple sources of advantage still = roll 2d20 once. Same for disadvantage.
- Common DM use: reward clever play with advantage; make desperate or poorly-positioned actions use disadvantage.

### Inspiration
- A reward the DM gives a player for exceptional roleplay, creative problem-solving, or embodying their character's personality/ideals/bonds/flaws.
- A player with Inspiration can spend it to gain Advantage on one d20 roll, or give it to another player.
- A player can hold only one Inspiration at a time.
- DM tip: give it freely, especially with new players. It signals what good roleplaying looks like at your table.

### Short Rest & Long Rest
- **Short Rest:** 1 hour of light activity. Players can spend Hit Dice (d + CON modifier) to regain HP. Some class features (e.g., Warlock spell slots, Fighter's Second Wind) recharge on short rest.
- **Long Rest:** 8 hours of sleep/light activity. Regains all HP, all Hit Dice up to half max, all spell slots, and most class features.
- **DM pacing note:** Long rests are powerful. In a dungeon or time-pressured scenario, preventing easy long rests raises stakes. One or two short rests per adventuring day is the intended rhythm.

## Running Combat

### Initiative
- Everyone rolls Dex check (not saving throw) — ties broken by higher Dex modifier, then DM discretion
- Monsters can share initiative (one roll per monster type, or one roll per group)
- Keep the order visible to players — reduces "wait, who goes?" friction

### Monster HP
- Use average HP from stat block for consistency (easier to track)
- Adjust HP on the fly if combat is running long or short — players don't know the stat block

### When Players Die
- 0 HP → death saves (DC 10 raw d20 roll — no modifiers added, not a Con save)
- 3 successes = stable; 3 failures = dead (doesn't have to be consecutive)
- A natural 20 = regain 1 HP and regain consciousness; a natural 1 = counts as 2 failures
- Taking damage at 0 HP = 1 death save failure; crit = 2 failures
- Stabilized creature stays unconscious with 1 HP
- Killing blow on unconscious creature = instant death
- Be honest about lethality — fudge sparingly and only to prevent unfun outcomes, not challenge

### Bounded Accuracy
- Proficiency bonus scales: +2 (levels 1-4), +3 (5-8), +4 (9-12), +5 (13-16), +6 (17-20)
- At level 3: proficiency is +2. Most DCs should be 10-15 for normal tasks, 20 for hard, 25 for extraordinary.
- Player ACs at level 3 are typically 14-17.
- Don't always tell players what DC they need — "roll it and see" preserves tension.

## NPC Design Principles

### The Three Things Every NPC Needs
1. **A Want** — what do they want right now, today?
2. **A Tell** — one physical or vocal habit that makes them memorable
3. **A Secret** — something they're not saying (doesn't have to be sinister)

### NPC Voice Differentiation
- Vocabulary level: flowery/formal vs blunt/crude
- Speech rhythm: fast/nervous vs slow/deliberate
- What they talk about unprompted (their obsession)
- What they never mention (their wound)

### Making NPCs Feel Real
- NPCs have opinions about other NPCs (ask "how does Barkeep feel about the blacksmith?")
- NPCs want things from the party, not just the reverse
- Memorable NPCs are usually quirky in one very specific way, not generically mysterious

## Scene Description Formula

A strong read-aloud description hits:
1. **First impression** — what do they see/feel immediately on entering?
2. **One unexpected detail** — something specific that makes this place feel real
3. **An invitation to interact** — something that implies there's more to explore

Keep it to 3-5 sentences. Players will interrupt — that's good. Aim to finish on a hook.

**Senses to cycle through:** Sight, sound, smell, temperature/texture (taste rarely)

**Avoid:** Listing everything in the room. Trust players to ask about details.

## Encounter Design

### Difficulty at Level 3 (4-5 players)
- **Easy:** ~150 XP total
- **Medium:** ~300 XP total
- **Hard:** ~450 XP total
- **Deadly:** ~600+ XP total

### XP Multipliers (by number of monsters)
- 2 monsters: ×1.5
- 3-6 monsters: ×2
- 7-10 monsters: ×2.5

### Beyond Combat
The best encounters have at least one non-combat option or sub-goal:
- An object to interact with (lever, altar, trapped chest)
- An NPC to talk down or threaten
- An environmental element (fire spreading, collapsing floor, reinforcements arriving)

## DM Philosophy Notes

### Rule of Fun Over Rules
When a rule creates a fun moment → apply it.
When a rule would kill a fun moment → rule in favor of the narrative, explain later if needed.
This is especially true for first-time players.

### "Yes, And" vs "No"
Prefer "yes, and" or "yes, but" over hard no's. If a player wants to do something creative:
- Can it succeed with a risk? → Set a DC
- Does it not make sense? → Explain why and offer an alternative
- Is it going to ruin things? → Only then consider no

### Spotlight Distribution
After each session, mentally review: did each player get a moment to shine?
If someone went quiet, find a hook for next session that speaks to their character specifically.

### Pacing
- Combat: Keep turns moving. If a player is taking >60 seconds, ask "what does your character do?"
- Roleplay: Let scenes breathe but watch for player disengagement cues
- Exploration: Give 2-3 interesting details per area, then let players drive
