# Loot Tool Ideas

1. "Smart" Party Filtering

Random loot tables often generate items the party can't actually use. If the party includes a Bladesinger wizard who relies on a rapier and light armor, it’s a major letdown when the boss drops a heavy +2 Greataxe.

    The Feature: Add checkboxes for the party's classes or preferred weapon types.

    How it Works: When the app rolls on the magic item table, you can filter out weapon and armor types that clash with the party's proficiencies, ensuring the drops are actually exciting. You could even have a specific "Generate Spellbook" button that automatically rolls 1d4 appropriate spells for the wizard to copy.

2. Thematic Loot Conversions

It breaks immersion when players defeat a pack of wild wolves and somehow find 45 gold pieces in the dirt.

    The Feature: A dropdown for "Monster Type" (e.g., Beast, Undead, Humanoid, Dragon).

    How it Works: The calculator rolls the standard CR gold value, but then converts that value into thematic items. Instead of 45 gold from a wolf, the tool outputs: "Pristine Wolf Pelts (Value: 20gp) and a rare beast gall bladder used in alchemy (Value: 25gp)." For undead, it might generate tarnished silver jewelry or antique coins.

3. Lore & Relic Generation

Finding a "Ruby worth 500gp" is fine, but finding a unique artifact is memorable.

    The Feature: A flavor text generator for art objects and gems.

    How it Works: You can build a small dictionary of historical eras, visual traits, and materials. Instead of a generic gem, the tool outputs: "A fist-sized, glowing red ruby set in tarnished brass, dating back to the Age of Arcanum. It is slightly warm to the touch (Value: 500gp)." It gives the DM instant world-building material.

4. The "Appraisal & Fence" Mechanic

Whenever players find loot, the very next question is usually, "Where can we sell this and for how much?"

    The Feature: Alongside the "True Value" of the item, display a "Merchant Offer" and a "Fenced Offer."

    How it Works: Apply a simple mathematical modifier. A legitimate merchant might offer 50% of the true value, while a shady black-market fence might offer 30% but won't ask questions about who it belonged to. This saves the DM from doing on-the-fly math while roleplaying a shopkeeper.

# Encounter Tool Ideas

1. The Action Economy Gauge

The single biggest factor in D&D combat isn't hit points; it's the number of actions taken per round. A party of four fighting a single boss monster will often obliterate it simply because they get four turns for every one of the boss's turns. Conversely, a party fighting 15 weak skeletons might get overwhelmed by sheer volume.

    The Feature: Add an "Action Economy Ratio" metric.

    How it Works: The tool compares the total number of actions the players have against the total number of monster attacks/actions. If the ratio goes above 2:1 in either direction, the UI displays a warning flag: "Warning: Party heavily outnumbered" or "Warning: Boss is vulnerable to being locked down—consider adding minions or Legendary Actions."

2. Magic Item / Optimization Drift Sliders

The official CR math assumes a party has no magic items and uses average tactics. If your friend's group has a ton of magic weapons or plays highly optimized character builds, standard encounters will feel like a cakewalk.

    The Feature: Add a "Party Power Level" toggle or slider (e.g., Standard, High Magic, Highly Optimized).

    How it Works: When toggled up, the tool artificially inflates the party's "XP Budget" by a set percentage (e.g., +15% for High Magic). This lets the DM know they can push past the standard "High Difficulty" cap without instantly causing a Total Party Kill.





3. Environmental Hazard Modifiers

A fight against four goblins in an empty room is easy. A fight against four goblins who are shooting arrows from behind total cover across a river of lava is deadly.

    The Feature: A checklist for "Battlefield Conditions" (e.g., Enemy has High Ground, Difficult Terrain limits melee, Environmental Hazards present).

    How it Works: Checking these boxes doesn't change the official XP math, but it adds a visual "Difficulty Bump" to the dashboard, reminding the DM that the terrain is acting as a "phantom monster" in the fight.
